You've probably seen a cellular automaton. A grid of identical cells, each running the same simple rule based on its neighbours. Out of those local rules, complex global patterns emerge — gliders, oscillators, sometimes even computation.

A few years ago, people started asking: what if those rules were learned, instead of hand-designed? That's the idea behind Neural Cellular Automata — you train a small neural network, and you run a copy of it inside every cell.

But there's a strange thing about that setup. The neural network is doing the learning. The cellular automaton is just the stage it performs on. The grid itself learns nothing.

So here's the question we asked instead. What if you flip it? What if the cellular automaton is the neural network — and the grid dynamics themselves do the learning?
