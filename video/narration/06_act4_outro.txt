Two reasons to care about this.

The first is silicon. Every operation in this system — the integrator, the tanh, the local trace update — maps cleanly onto a standard analog crossbar primitive. Nothing here needs a G P U. The forward pass is physics. The backward pass is more physics. The learning rule is a few capacitors and a multiplier per cell. And now we know which parts of that circuit need to be precise — the backward message readout — and which parts can be sloppy and cheap.

That's a co-design result. Most neuromorphic hardware papers tell you their architecture is hardware-friendly because it's parallelisable. So is a matrix multiply. The interesting question is whether learning itself can be local, on the chip, without an external optimiser. For this grid, the answer is yes — within a precision budget we can now quantify.

The second reason is more speculative, but worth saying. Cortex is a roughly planar sheet of nearly-identical units, doing local computation, with most of its connectivity confined to nearby cells. We're not claiming the brain works like this grid. But the brain has to solve a similar problem — credit assignment, locally, on a topology that doesn't admit a clean backward pass. Knowing which classes of noise break local learning, and which don't, is at least a useful constraint on the kinds of mechanisms that could plausibly be doing it.

What's next? The fragility result points somewhere specific. If the backward message is the failure mode, you want infrastructure that broadcasts it cleanly over long distances — something like a hierarchical projection layered on top of the grid. In the brain, that role is played by subcortical structures. In silicon, it could be a tree of routing buffers above the grid plane. But that's the next paper. For now: a continuous cellular automaton learns to read handwritten digits, and we know what it would take to put it on a chip.
